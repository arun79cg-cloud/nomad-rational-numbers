Why do we need rational numbers?

We know natural numbers (1,2,3…) and whole numbers (0,1,2…). But x+18=5 needs x=−13, not a whole number — so we use integers.

Even integers fall short. 2x=3 needs x=3/2, not an integer. So we extend to rational numbers.

On the board

Natural → Whole → Integer → Rational

Each new set lets us solve more equations.

What is a rational number?

Any number written as p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0.

Whole numbers and integers are also rational: 4=4/1, −7=−7/1, 0=0/1.

Examples

Rational: 3/5, −7/2, 0, 4, −11

NOT rational: √2, π

Teach · Step 3 of 9

Closure property

A set is closed under an operation if the result always stays in the same set.

Rational numbers

3/5 + 4/5 = 7/5 → rational ✔

−2/3 × 9/4 = −3/2 → rational ✔

Closed under: addition, subtraction, multiplication.

NOT closed under division (÷ 0 is undefined).

Teach · Step 4 of 9

Commutativity & associativity

Commutative: a+b=b+a and a×b=b×a.

Associative: a+(b+c)=(a+b)+c and a×(b×c)=(a×b)×c.

For rational numbers

Addition → commutative ✔ associative ✔

Multiplication → commutative ✔ associative ✔

Subtraction, division → neither ✘

Teach · Step 5 of 9

Role of 0 and 1

Additive identity: 0. For any rational a, a+0=a.

Multiplicative identity: 1. For any rational a, a×1=a.

Remember

0 keeps a number same under addition.

1 keeps a number same under multiplication.

Teach · Step 6 of 9

Additive inverse & reciprocal

Additive inverse of a is −a, because a+(−a)=0. Example: inverse of 3/4 is −3/4.

Reciprocal of a/b is b/a, because (a/b)×(b/a)=1. Example: reciprocal of 3/5 is 5/3.

Examples

Additive inverse of −5/7 is 5/7.

Reciprocal of −4 is −1/4.

Teach · Step 7 of 9

Distributive property

Multiplication distributes over addition and subtraction.

Rule

a × (b+c) = a×b + a×c

a × (b−c) = a×b − a×c

Example: (3/5)(10+5) = 6+3 = 9

Teach · Step 8 of 9

On the number line

Every rational number has a place on the number line. To place p/q, divide the gap between two integers into q equal parts and count p parts.

Example

Plot 3/4: divide 0 to 1 into 4 parts. Third mark = 3/4.

Plot −5/2: go left of −2 by half a unit.

Teach · Step 9 of 9

Between two rational numbers

Between any two rational numbers there are infinitely many more rational numbers.

Simple method: take the average. If a and b are rational, then (a+b)/2 lies between them.

Example

Between 1/2 and 3/4:

(1/2 + 3/4) ÷ 2 = (5/4) ÷ 2 = 5/8

5/8 lies between 1/2 and 3/4 ✔
