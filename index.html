Why do we need rational numbers?

We already know natural numbers (1, 2, 3 …) and whole numbers (0, 1, 2, 3 …). They help us count and do many simple sums.

But some equations do not have solutions inside these sets. For example, the equation x + 18 = 5 needs x = −13, which is not a whole number. That made us bring in integers.

Even integers are not enough. The equation 2x = 3 needs x = 3/2. This is not an integer. So we extend our number system once more and introduce rational numbers.

Each new set helps us solve more equations.

What is a rational number?

A rational number is any number that can be written in the form p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0.

Whole numbers and integers are also rational numbers because we can write them with denominator 1. For example, 4 = 4/1, 0 = 0/1, −7 = −7/1.

Examples

3/5, −7/2, 0, 4, −11 are all rational numbers.

Numbers like √2 or π cannot be written as p/q with integers.

Teach • Step 3 of 9

Closure property

A set is closed under an operation if we start with numbers from the set and the result is also in the set.

Rational numbers

3/5 + 4/5 = 7/5 → rational ✔

−2/3 × 9/4 = −3/2 → rational ✔

So rational numbers are closed under addition and multiplication.

Subtraction is also closed. But division is not closed because a ÷ 0 is not defined.

Teach • Step 4 of 9

Commutativity & associativity

Commutative means we can change the order: a + b = b + a or a × b = b × a.

Associative means we can change the grouping: a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c and a × (b × c) = (a × b) × c.

For rational numbers

Addition: commutative and associative.

Multiplication: commutative and associative.

Subtraction and division: neither commutative nor associative.

Teach • Step 5 of 9

The role of 0 and 1 (identities)

Zero acts as the additive identity. For any rational number a, we have a + 0 = a = 0 + a.

One acts as the multiplicative identity. For any rational number a, we have a × 1 = a = 1 × a.

Identity recap

0 keeps the number same under addition.

1 keeps the number same under multiplication.

Teach • Step 6 of 9

Additive inverse & reciprocal

The additive inverse of a number a is the number which gives 0 when added to a. For 3/4 it is −3/4.

The multiplicative inverse (or reciprocal) of a rational number a/b (a, b ≠ 0) is b/a, because (a/b) × (b/a) = 1.

Examples

Additive inverse of −5/7 is 5/7.

Reciprocal of 3/5 is 5/3; reciprocal of −4 is −1/4.

Teach • Step 7 of 9

Distributive property

Multiplication is distributive over addition and subtraction. For rational numbers:

Rule

a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c

a × (b − c) = a × b − a × c

We use this to make calculations easier.

Example: 3/5 × (10 + 5) = 3/5 × 10 + 3/5 × 5 = 6 + 3 = 9. This is easier than first adding and then multiplying big numbers.

Teach • Step 8 of 9

Rational numbers on a number line

Every rational number can be shown on a number line. To place p/q, we divide the distance between integers into equal parts.

Example

To plot 3/4, look between 0 and 1 and divide into 4 equal parts. The third mark from 0 is 3/4.

To plot −5/2, go to −2 and then move half a unit to the left.

Quick check

Teach • Step 9 of 9

Rational numbers between two rational numbers

Between any two different rational numbers, we can always find many more rational numbers.

One simple method: take the average of the two numbers. If a and b are rational, then (a + b)/2 is a rational number between them.

Example

Between 1/2 and 3/4:

(1/2 + 3/4)/2 = (5/4)/2 = 5/8, which lies between them.
